#### The Crippled God by Steven Erikson

⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️  
Series: Malazan Book of the Fallen #10
**Started:** 2026-01-05  
**Finished:** 2026-01-15  

The conclusion to such an epic work defies a review that is succinct. The first—and most important—question I have to ask is: _Did Erikson deserve the trust I placed in him upon finishing_ **Dust of Dreams**? The answer is yes.  

In many ways, _The Crippled God_ is the archetypal _Malazan Book of the Fallen_ novel: a plot filled with symbolism, complex motivations, and clashing perspectives—rarely explained to the reader. The novel can be read on three levels:  

1. A fast-paced fantasy novel filled with the classic tropes (excellently done) of **Gods**, **Monsters**, **Good vs. Evil**, **Magic**, and **Dragons**—epic battles and tragic sacrifice.  
2. A beautifully crafted world of vast and intricately drawn landscapes, cultures, peoples, and characters.  
3. A treatise in moral philosophy that weighs what is God, what is love, and what either means without compassion.  

On 1 and 2, Erikson completely succeeds. The action, world, and characters are beautifully drawn, and the pace frantic. We are drawn with the **Bonehunters** across the desert, feeling their thirst and desperation. We feel their world and their pain, and the action is viscerally rendered with all the gore, violence, and horror of war. The withholding of **Tavore’s** inner life until the final chapters continues to work excellently as a storytelling technique. She is the canvas for others’ projections and becomes the vehicle through which we come to understand the motivations and beliefs of others. This is particularly striking and poignant.  

On 3, Erikson almost—but not completely—succeeds. _The Crippled God_ and the attempt to right the injustices piled upon Him form a great and noble cause. Yet I’m left with unease: is the pain and suffering of one being—no matter how great—worth the death and torture of thousands? Is Erikson suggesting that the little people should be sacrificed for their betters?  

A utilitarian argument is not the only way to critique Erikson’s moral message. He plays with the “Great Men” theory of history, with **Tavore**, **Shadowthrone**, and **Cotillion** as the “Great Men.” But what of the little people used as pawns to right this wrong? You could extend this to a Marxist criticism: the proletariat—the **Bonehunters** and others—are sacrificed by the bourgeois (**Tavore**) for the nobility (**The Crippled God**, etc.).  

There is, however, a counterbalance. The Gods are shown as greedy, selfish, and venal—but also as the creations of a fallen humanity. It is humanity’s task to right the wrongs as best they can. Justice depends on good people doing the right thing for the right reasons. **Tavore** and the **Bonehunters** are the ones who decide to act, and through them, Erikson reasserts faith in the power of compassion and agency.  

Even with these moral tensions unresolved, the ambition and coherence of Erikson’s vision remain extraordinary.  

As a piece of literature and a great story, the _Malazan Book of the Fallen_ is a masterpiece. It is fantasy, and it is literary fiction. In scope, scale, and structure, it can be compared to another great work of literature—Dante’s _Divine Comedy_: an epic work of morality, with **Gods**, **Monsters**, and **Angels**; the just and the unjust. The themes of redemption, faith, and love are comparable.  

One final note: the poetry at the beginning of parts and chapters didn’t really work for me. This may well be a personal failing. I’m not comfortable with poetry and find it hard to appreciate, though I recognize it as part of Erikson’s attempt to give the saga a mythic and elegiac tone.  

<a id="entry-2026-01-the-light-of-impossible-stars-f2975c63"></a>
#### The Light of Impossible Stars by Gareth L. Powell

Series: Embers of War 3
Started: 2026-01-16
Finished: 2026-01-18
⭐️⭐️⭐️

The concluding chapter in Powell’s space opera is competent and engaging with good world building and excellent pace. Short sharp chapters and shifting perspectives, but to my mind, unexceptional. In this final instalment, Powell crystallises the themes of individuality and moral choice: it is the actions and choices that make a person—human or otherwise. Organisations that seek to strip away that agency are suspect. As one passage puts it:

“I had seen firsthand what happened when men and women surrender their individuality to the state, when they allowed themselves to become pawns in someone else's games.”

Powell delivers a broadly consistent moral message, but its force is weakened by the character of Cordelia Pa. Subsumed into the mind of the Plates, she at first chooses to fight, only to later concede—to flee, to do what she was built for. This contrasts with Troubledog, who explicitly chooses not to do what she was built for. Is it therefore acceptable to surrender individuality for a good cause, but a moral imperative to reclaim it against a bad one? If so, what is truly at stake is not individuality itself, but the definition of good and bad. The architecture of the argument falters there.

I also had structural issues with The Light of Impossible Stars. Powell’s shifting perspectives, which I have enjoyed in earlier books, feel inconsistent here—slipping between first and third person. Some characters’ voices are distinct; others blur. Sal and Troubledog’s characterisation stood out as the strongest.

Finally, the deus ex machina of Cordelia Powell felt unnecessary. It created an overly neat ending, allowing Powell to tie up the story too easily, without much compromise. I was left feeling there were many unexplored possibilities—sabotage, persuasion, alliance-building with other races. The tidy resolution seemed like a missed opportunity.

Overall, an enjoyable but unchallenging read. It didn’t teach me much or leave a lasting aftertaste—perfect, perhaps, for a holiday, but not one that will linger long in memory.

#### I, Claudius by Robert Graves

Series: Claudius 1
Started: 2026-01-18
Finished: 2026-01-26
⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️

This is a work of profound scholarship and ambition and ultimately art. I believe _I, Claudius_ is an inspiration for other authors of historical fiction I have read (Hilary Mantel & James Clavels) who share his intimate style and focus on motivations and inner lives of their characters. I strongly believe that Christopher Ruocchio was influenced by Graves. His Empire is modeled on ancient Rome and we see that world of intrigue war and violence through Hadrian's narration of his own autobiography.

I'm left wondering if "Claudius" is a reliable narrator and if Graves meant him to be perceived as such. Graves paints Claudius as a virtuous man according to the norms of a patrician of his time. But even by the standards of the early-mid 20th Century (when Graves was writing) Claudius is a monster. He keeps slaves, is indifferent to his own children, and delights in the suffering of Gladiators and animals at the games.

It is hard for any modern historian or author to separate myth, propoganda and fact. I believe that Graves relies heavily on "The Life of the Ceasars" by Suetonius. I have not read this but understand it to be, with Tacitus, one of the only near contemporaneous sources.

The style is dense with facts, names and dates thrown at one constantly. Though Graves mostly succeeds in maintaining pace and intrigue. I will certainly read _Claudius the God_. I'm hoping we see more hypocrisy and self justification from Graves' Claudius and we see a little more of the internal life of the Paletine hill and palace.

<a id="entry-2026-01-the-midnight-library-3491a923"></a>
#### The Midnight Library by Matt Haig

⭐️⭐️
Started: 2026-01-26
Finished: 2026-02-01
#bookclub

This novel has been extremely successful, attracting praise from celebrities and broadsheet newspapers such as The Guardian and The Times. It is firmly situated in the realm of popular literary fiction: accessible, reassuring, and undemanding. It asks very little of its reader, and its ambitions are correspondingly modest. The moral is universal, the metaphors are clichéd, and the prose lacks subtlety:

> …she was. A black hole. A dying star collapsing in on itself.
> The world’s smallest violin playing.

The plot follows a familiar structure, repeating both the moral logic and narrative shape of It’s a Wonderful Life, with Mrs Elm occupying the role of the guiding angel. An unhappy person attempts suicide and is granted the opportunity to glimpse alternative lives in order to learn the value of the one she has abandoned.

> “Happy moments can turn into pain given time.”

These lives, however, are conspicuously without jeopardy. The early episodes — in Australia, in the Cotswolds pub, in Svalbard — function as illustrative sketches rather than genuine explorations of choice and consequence. They teach Nora very little, and they ask even less of the reader. It is only when she arrives at her life with Ash that the novel gestures towards emotional weight, yet even here the execution feels thin. Her love for her daughter is incidental and superficially rendered; I did not feel the wrench or depth such a relationship demands. The relationship with Ash is similarly hurried, sketched just deeply enough to serve the moral before the narrative moves on.

Despite this, the novel is effective. On finishing it, I found myself making a renewed vow not to take my life for granted. But this effectiveness comes easily. The sentiment is so broad, the moral so universal, that eliciting an emotional response feels like the minimum achievement rather than a mark of distinction. I felt the message, but I was not inspired by it.

A truly great novel would have forced me to confront my own regrets and choices, not merely reassured me of the value of life in the abstract. The Midnight Library offers comfort rather than confrontation, affirmation rather than disturbance. I am not convinced that this is a failure so much as an intention: to unsettle the reader too deeply might compromise the novel’s commercial appeal. As it stands, this is a perfectly pleasant beach read — soothing, sincere, and emotionally legible — but not much more.

#### Blindsight by Peter Watts

⭐️⭐️
Series: Firefall 1
Started: 2026-02-02
Finished: 2026-02-05

This is a novel filled with ideas. the nature of sentience, cociousness and free will. The question asked _is sentience really all that great - is it an advantage or a drawback - can an inteligent being be unaware?_  These themes are compelling and are subtly worked in from the begining with Siri's awareness of his world irreparably changed by his radical hemispherectomy at as a child.

There are three _"aliens"_ in Blindsight. First there is Siri. His role as _"Synthesist"_ means he is always on the edge of the groups he works with, an outsider by neccessity. He is an alien by human standards having to use algoirthms and patterns to navigate through the human social world as a Chineese Box. He interprets the world without any understanding of it, just as he interprets the augmented humans he is sent to observe and explain. Second is Jukka, the Vampire, who is feared as much as he is needed. Jukka is the alien in the midst of the Theseus. Too different to be accepted by Siri or the crew of savants.

Then of course there is the Rorschach and the scamblers. They have inscrutable motives, just as Jukka and Siri, are deadly (as Jukka is) and live in a strange and alien world to the crew (just as Siri does). The question implied by Watts is if the Rorschach and the scamblers are any more alien than humans are given circumstances and perspective. The only characters who really understand what is happening are Jukka and the scramplers (with Siri at the end) - the concious human savants are blind.

I found Watt's style full of sharp edges. The continual use of specialist terminiology had me reaching for the dictionary constantly and then Wikipedia when the OED failed me. This broke my flow and took me out of Watts' world. I can't help but be left with a problem with the Watts' style over the substance of his writting. The sentences felt "stuttery" and lacking in eligence. I haven't got the knowledge and critical skill to pin point the source of this frustration - but I felt this as I read. This did not fit with the character of Siri. He is supposed to be able to explain ideas and produce reports that are easily understood by the elite of his world. Surely then he should be a better writter? The plot jerked along with flashbacks into Siri's past littered throughout. While providing welcome background and character development they did not fit with the narrative conciet that Watts has chosen. Why would Siri include this in his account? What difference would this make to his audience at home - how do they strengthen his warning to the people of Earth? A change in tactics here could have integrated this far better into the story. Jukka could taunt him with his past as he surely had read everything there is to know of his crew. Instead Watts chose the immediacy of a physical assault at the expense of the novels own internal coherence.

A novel then with great ambition stumbling in the execution. I'm not put off by Watts, his ideas make the effort of grappling with his prose worth while but I don't believe he will feature often on my bookshelves.

#### Assassin's Quest by Robin Hobb

⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️
Series: Realm of the Elderlings 3
Series: Farseer Trilogy 3
Started: 2026-02-21
Finished: 2026-02-28

I could not put this down. I read and read — pushing myself well past bedtimes and ignoring almost everything else. I felt the world drawn by Hobb viscerally, and I felt the pain, hate, and love that swim through this book like the current of a pulling river. The frustration of love denied, the acceptance of loss, and the hope for the future.

Hobb's world is complex not just in terms of the bright and full landscape she has built, but in the characters — their motivations and their relationships to one another. Regal is a spoilt child but desperately afraid. Hobb shows that the Red Ships are not mindlessly seeking violence, but continuing a cycle of violence that the Six Duchies, in their own desperation, began. Hobb tackles sexual politics with the Fool and the conflict between themself and Starling with ease. The Fool is himself — and this is the most painful lesson of all. Where the Fool is irrepressibly himself, believing in and living his quest, Fitz is denied his true self — as a loving father and husband — his life is not and never has been his own. The Fool and Fitz can be seen as a literal representation of the double-edged sword of prophecy — the Fool gets what he wants; Fitz is always denied his heart’s desire.

There is also the contrast and tension between the Wit and the Skill. One can read a class dynamic into this as well. The Wit represents a power open to all (all, in some way, have it), and because it is open to all it is repressed and stigmatized by the powerful. It is a power of love, of building. The Skill is the preserve of the Farseers and the nobility; as such, its practice is associated with high status and jealously protected. It is a thing of bright power, able to kill and to inflict dominion on others. Where the Skill is a power of domination, the Wit is a power of bonding and joining — it creates empathy. In Fitz this contrast is most clear. He is controlled and compelled by Verity through the Skill, and loved and nurtured by Nighteyes via the Wit.

The Dragons, or Elderlings, are an interesting creation — monsters, in a way. Requiring the complete sacrifice of identity and life to be born, the complete subjugation of self to power. In a way it is fitting that a king do this for his people, whom he subjugates. There is also the Stone Garden of slumbering beasts desperate to feed and consume life. Are they any different from any other carnivore? Or are they something more sinister? That is a question I cannot yet answer.

Hobb has created a rich world full of realised characters that live, breathe, love, hate, and stink. In its scale it also feels intimate and human. She does this with a gesture, a sigh, the smell of a worn shirt, and the funk of a forest. I can see why the Realm of the Elderlings is loved.

<a id="entry-2026-03-the-left-hand-of-darkness-f9fe27fd"></a>
#### The Left Hand of Darkness by Ursula K. Le Guin

⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️
Started: 2026-03-01
Finished: 2026-03-08
#bookclub

The Left Hand of Darkness is an interesting book. It uses a science fiction premise as a means to conduct a thought experiment — what if gender wasn’t the organizing principle of society? It succeeds as a critique of gender roles. Ai, the Envoy, only superficially examines his own latent misogyny. His fundamental assumptions about masculinity and strength, and femininity (effeminacy) as weakness, are woven through the narrative. His failure to confront this makes the message more poignant, though on a casual read this nuance could easily be lost.

Le Guin critiques masculinity acidly — through the lens of physicality and pride.

> He, after all, had no standards of manliness, of virility, to complicate his pride.

The use of pronouns and their associations is important; the use of “she” and “he” feels deliberate. Ai conflates femininity (“effeminacy”) with weakness throughout, providing a view of Ai’s ingrained chauvinism. Can he not conceive of a person as a “she” without lowering them in his eyes?

> ...to my eyes effeminate—not in the sense of delicacy, etc., but in just the opposite sense: a gross, bland fleshiness, a bovinity without point or edge.

The image of femininity as cow-like is both horrible and resonant. Cows bear calves, they nourish us with their milk, they are stolid, perhaps stupid, and are chattel. Is this how Ai sees all women? Ai tells us that he has never really thought about women and their perspective.

> “Tell me, how does the other sex of your race differ from yours?”
> “...I never thought of that,”
> “...In a sense, women are more alien to me than you are.”

He has, and continues to be, wilfully blind to the female — not rejecting it outright, but failing to conceptualise or consider a feminine perspective, dismissing it as beneath contempt. The fact that this view is hidden, latent in Ai's psyche, is a real achievement of Le Guin. A lesser writer would have made this obvious. The damning thing is that Ai is otherwise a great and model human being. Ai's prejudice isn't monstrous; it's horrific for being ordinary.

This was, for me, the most evocative and resonant sentence in the novel. It describes the privilege of masculinity and the horror (that I share) of losing it:

> One is respected and judged only as a human being. It is an appalling experience.

Here Le Guin hits the nail on the head. To be stripped of the societal protection of masculinity and be judged only by the content of your character is deeply uncomfortable. It forces one to really ask what it means to be human and where one's “worth” as a human being is derived.

I found Le Guin's introduction the most interesting aspect of the novel. Her discussions on truth, analogy, and the purpose of sci-fi, literature, and art were the most intellectually engaging aspects for me.

> and then when they [writers of fiction] are done writing down this pack of lies, they say, There! That’s the truth!

and later,

> Distrust everything I say. I am telling the truth. The only truth I can understand or express is, logically defined, a lie. Psychologically defined, a symbol. Aesthetically defined, a metaphor.

This gets very close to Hilary Mantel's justification, in her 2017 Reith Lectures, of the truth in historical fiction. By inhabiting another person we come to empathise in a way with the other that tells a greater truth.

In this vein Le Guin writes:

> In fact, while we read a novel, we are insane — bonkers. We believe in the existence of people who aren’t there, we hear their voices, we watch the battle of Borodino with them, we may even become Napoleon. Sanity returns (in most cases) when the book is closed.

She sums up the whole beauty of reading fiction — the “double speak” that one most willingly engages in — beautifully.

> In reading a novel, any novel, we have to know perfectly well that the whole thing is nonsense, and then, while reading, believe every word of it.

And finally this most important point:

> The novelist says in words what cannot be said in words.

This introduction set my expectations for The Left Hand of Darkness very high indeed. My expectation of greater internal conflict in Ai was a result of wanting a crisis of conscience that would reveal to Ai that his gendered view of the world has blinded him. I wanted him struck dumb with the realisation.

But on reflection I see Le Guin's subtlety. He is able to empathise with one person (Estraven) and even come to love them. But he can't see females of his own people with clear and unbiased eyes. He is locked forever to repeat the mistake of his culture — failing to judge all as simply human beings.

<a id="entry-2026-03-ship-of-magic-4845b7fb"></a>
#### Ship of Magic by Robin Hobb

⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️

Series: Realm of the Elderlings 4
Series: Liveship Traders Trilogy 1
Started: 2026-03-08
Finished: 2026-03-29

Another engaging and thrilling story from the world of the Elderlings. *Ship of Magic* demonstrates Hobb's versatility as a writer. The use of the first person in *The Farseer Trilogy* is suited to the claustrophobic world of court politics and personal sacrifice. In *Ship of Magic*, Hobb needs to broaden her readers' horizon. A first-person perspective would not have worked on the wide open seas. The multiple perspectives used give a sense of scale and grandeur to Hobb's world that was implied in *Farseer* but are drawn vividly in the world of Althea and Vivacia.

Hobb sows doubt in her readers' mind, reflecting the crushing loss of confidence that Althea feels. At first, we are made to doubt Althea's perspective. Is perhaps Kyle's view of Althea correct? Is she just a spoilt daddy's girl with no real skill or understanding? It seems plausible, after all. But as we see Kyle's need for control become more than a man doing what is necessary to save his family fortune—becoming something insidious, dominating, and aggressive—we understand that Althea was right. Hobb takes her reader on a parallel journey of doubt to Althea.

Wintrow and Althea are opposite, but both are used by Hobb to show different ways to self-actualisation and mastery of self. Althea loves the sea and does all she can to become a good sailor—to prove to herself, and to Kyle, that she is more than a captain's daughter. Wintrow has a more complex journey of selfhood. He must understand who he is and what his purpose in life is. Is he a boy, a man, a priest, or a sailor? What is right and what is wrong? Hobb uses Wintrow to explore morality. Where Althea acts, Wintrow thinks.

The liveships are an interesting concept, tied to the idea of sentience, selfhood, and freedom. Are they a tool owned by a family or a member of that family? Pets or equals? Slaves or willing brothers and sisters? This is never resolved, and I see this becoming an important theme in the remainder of the *Liveship Traders*.

It took almost a month to read *Ship of Magic*, not from a lack of enjoyment but due to distraction. Life simply got in the way. That meant I had time to linger and digest Hobb's world rather than devouring it; I savored it. Hobb is well on the way to becoming one of my favorite writers. Her imagination and ability to weave character, philosophy, morality, and ambiguity all together set her apart.

#### Civilizations by Laurent Binet

⭐️⭐️⭐️
Started: 2026-03-30
Finished: 2026-04-03
#bookclub

This book is a work of speculative historical fiction. Imagining a world where it was the Inca and the Aztecs that invaded and conquered Europe rather than Spain, Portugal and Britain who took the Americas.

Binet deploys irony with a heavy hand. It is Atahualpa who captures the Spanish King and Holy Roman Emperor rather than Pizarro capturing Atahualpa. The tactics that Atahualpa used in *Civilizations* is exactly the same as those used by the conquistadors in Peru. The accidental death of Charles also mirrors the real events in Peru.

Binet also critiques Christianity. His portrayal of Luther is extremely accurate and the intolerances of the civil and religious environment of the 16th century are well drawn and accurate. That said his portrayal of the Atahualpa and the cult of the Sun as benign and enlightened takes a great deal of latitude. In the land of the Four Quarters all non-nobles held no property, wore the same clothes and had very little freedom. This is not explored or even discussed in the novel. This uncritical view of the Inca civilisation is potentially sentimental. This may be a deliberate choice. The chronicler is culturally Inca - are they lionising their own culture and is this a subtle way for Binet to critique the practice of history as a whole: "history is written by the victor". If so I feel this is too subtle. The lack of contradiction makes me feel it is more likely Binet has romanticized the culture of the Inca. It is easy to fall into this trap when considering the cultures destroyed and the horrors inflicted by Pizzarro and the conquistadors in general. Binet does not give the Aztecs the same treatment. Not surprising given their love of human sacrifice. That is so clearly beyond the pale for even the most committed moral relativist.

The conceit used of a chronicler recording the events of the conquest of the Europe worked well. However, the first few chapters covering the events of arrival of the Danes felt a very different style. Binet seemed to drift into the voice of a chronicler later. This gave the novel a shifting voice that felt a little abrupt to me. I was confused, at first, if I was following different voices but of course I was not. This took me out of the immersion.

I saw this as an enjoyable historical fantasy more akin to Robert Harris' _Fatherland_ than a serious work of literature. I feel this novel was an indulgence of Binet's own cultural prejudices than an attempt to really consider the impact of collonialization. This sits in contrast to _Noughts & Crosses_ by Malorie Blackman which critically explored the nature and origin of contemporary racial prejudice and discrimination.
